ONI · Physics Field Guide
How great VR combat actually feels real — and how to learn it.

Every game we're building on solved one piece of the same problem: your controller is weightless, but the weapon must not be. This page is the distilled craft — the handful of mechanisms behind Blade & Sorcery's heavy steel, Alyx's hands, Thrill of the Fight's honest punches, SUPERHOT's time. The demos are live — use your mouse; feel the idea.

Concept 01 — the one that matters most

Your hand is a ghost. The sword is real.

The core trick of weighty VR melee: the blade is a real physics object with mass and inertia, and it is not glued to your hand — it chases your hand, pulled by a spring-damper (a "PD controller"). Move slowly and it obeys. Whip it and it lags, bends your arc, carries momentum — your brain reads that lag as weight. Blade & Sorcery is this idea, tuned for five years.

And damage is velocity-gated (Thrill of the Fight's law): a lazy push does nothing — the lantern just swings away. A committed cut takes it clean. Try to slice the lantern.

tip speed 0.0 m/s swing hard to cut — push = bounce

Why lag = weight

With no force feedback, the only channel left is visual dynamics. The delay and overshoot of a massive blade chasing your ghost-hand is a pseudo-haptic: your brain infers mass from motion it can see.

Why gate on velocity

If contact alone did damage, waggling would win. Gating on tip speed (and edge alignment) makes real technique — hip rotation, follow-through — the winning strategy. That's the workout, enforced by physics.

The tuning war

Too stiff a spring = weightless toy. Too soft = drunk noodle. The entire "feel" of a melee game lives in this one tuning curve — and it must be found by playtesting, never by reasoning.

Concept 02 — SUPERHOT's law, ONI's drink

Time belongs to the player.

SUPERHOT's whole genius is one line of code: world speed = your speed. Hold still and the fight waits for you; commit and it commits back. It converts panic into choreography — which is exactly how ONI's legendary drink works: the drunker you are, the more time eases, the more the night becomes a dance you can learn. Move your mouse to move time. Survive the volley.

world speed 0% the drink slider adds slow-mo + softens the frame — comfort disguised as an advantage

Why it's replayable

Deterministic choreography + player-owned time = mastery curve. The fight is the same; you are not. Run 30, you move like John Wick — that's the loop ONI is built on.

The drink, mechanically

Vignette strengthens (comfort), color richens (style), time eases (assist). Three dials, one diegetic object. The player never sees a settings menu — they see a drink.

Warm-up by design

Early waves run slower and telegraph longer — an athletic warm-up disguised as story pacing, so the body is ready when the night gets fast.

The Craft — what each game actually does

Four masters. One lesson each.

Pulled from developer commentary, GDC talks, SDK docs, and modder reverse-engineering — the real techniques, not reviews. Every one of these is buildable with today's engines.

Blade & Sorcery
Our sword model
The weapon is a real physics object that chases your hand — it's never glued on.
  • Spring / damper / maxForce joint between hand and blade. The force cap — not mass — is what makes a claymore feel unlike a dagger.
  • Linear stiff, rotational soft → the blade deflects in angle and drags along obstacles. That's why clashes read as fencing.
  • Velocity-gated damage + edge-alignment dot test. Taps bounce; a committed cut along the edge kills.
  • Penetration = collision swapped for a damped constraint — the wet drag of a stab is pure damper tuning. Most-praised sensation in the game.
  • Enemies are active ragdolls — muscle-springs pull limbs to the animation pose. "Knockdown" = turning the springs down.
Fletcher · BasSDK docs · KospY interviews
Half-Life: Alyx
Our gun model
Sell the hands, cut what has no feedback, make fumbles the player's fault.
  • Physics hands chase a "ghost" target; grab = snap to an authored pose, brief desync tolerated. A right-looking grip beats 1:1.
  • Multi-step reload on separate buttons (eject / insert / rack). Players blame themselves, not the game — playtesters' favorite moment.
  • Diegetic ammo on the weapon (grip LEDs), never a HUD. Reload-early keeps the round chambered — the gun rewards intimacy.
  • Recoil = model kick + haptic burst. Never the camera — in VR the camera is your head.
  • They CUT melee & two-handed guns rather than ship them half-convincing. One hand always free.
Valve Developer Commentary · Road to VR
Thrill of the Fight
Our hand-to-hand + workout
Measure what the body actually did. Reward form, not muscle. No game in between.
  • Auto-calibration: the game finds the hardest punch you can land reliably and makes that your max. Shoving = outlier = instant nerf.
  • Body Effort (TotF2): power graded on the whole chain — torso, weight shift, footwork. Arm-flicks grade low.
  • No lock-on, no health bars, no windows. Design test: does real boxing skill transfer? It does.
  • Blocking is pure geometry — limb volumes disarm the incoming fist. Free-form parry, zero parry system.
  • Your real body is the stamina meter. Fitness is emergent — 9–15 kcal/min, sparring-level.
Ian Fitz guide + threads · UploadVR · VR Health Inst.
The Field
SUPERHOT · Boneworks · Gorn · Until You Fall
Time, tone, and the one rule you can never break.
  • SUPERHOT: time couples to BODY motion (not head). Scanning is free; committing costs time. Slow-mo feels like skill. ← ONI's drink/loop.
  • Gorn proves tuning IS tone — same lag params, stiff = heft, loose = comedy. You can't borrow numbers across feels.
  • Until You Fall: feedback stacking — haptic + flash + SFX + kill slow-mo. Five weak channels ≈ the one (force) VR lacks.
  • The agency asymmetry: you can react to the player but never move the player. All forces on them must be diegetic.
  • Pseudo-haptics (science): render the virtual hand slightly slower than the real one → the brain perceives weight (Samad, CHI 2019).
GDC Vault · Game Developer · Voices of VR · ACM
The Curriculum

Eight concepts. Learn these and you can build weighty VR melee.

Ranked. The first one is 80% of the feel; the rest is tuning and honesty.

  1. Phantom/physical decoupling. Invisible tracked target + visible rigidbody weapon joined by springs. Every serious system is this.
  2. PD / spring-damper tuning as tone. The stiffness & damping constants ARE the game's personality. Asymmetric linear-vs-rotational is the pro move.
  3. Pseudo-haptics (control/display ratio). Why lag reads as mass, how far you can push it, and why you must coach the player to "pretend it's heavy."
  4. Velocity-gated damage. Momentum-at-contact + minimum thresholds + edge alignment. The anti-waggle law — your real muscles become the weight sim.
  5. Feedback stacking instead of force. Haptic pulse + SFX + VFX + world-side hit-stop + enemy flinch. Never freeze the camera.
  6. Active-ragdoll enemies. Spring-driven ragdolls tracking animation poses; impulse on hit; 0.1–0.3s blend to full ragdoll on death.
  7. The agency asymmetry. Never impose motion/knockback/hit-stop on the player's body. Governs your whole comfort/nausea budget.
  8. The resolution contract. Decide before tuning: total-on-contact (SUPERHOT), simulation (B&S), or telegraph/pattern (Until You Fall). Don't mix.
The Synthesis

What this means for ONI: Japan.

Every pillar maps to a proven, shipping technique. The one novel piece — the cloth — is answered below.

Sword
Blade & Sorcery's spring/damper/maxForce blade + velocity-gated edge damage + penetration-as-constraint. Tune stiff + damped for heavy katana heft. Proven recipe.
Gun
Alyx's laws exactly: one-handed, multi-step reload, diegetic ammo, recoil on the model + haptic. The "cost of the draw" is just Alyx's earned-reload gesture applied to concealment.
Hands
Thrill of the Fight's velocity + body-effort model with reliability-seeking auto-calibration. Form beats muscle; your body is the stamina meter = the workout.
The drink
SUPERHOT's time-couples-to-motion is the diegetic engine. Slowing time is BOTH the mastery aid AND a nausea reducer — the drink disguises an accessibility assist as an advantage.
Opponents
Active-ragdoll bodies + damage-driven reactions + distinct exploitable styles. Matches "each fighter is a school of the sword, same move each run" exactly.
The floor
The agency asymmetry is the hard constraint: the fight comes to the player at a station; we never shove the camera. Room-scale footwork is the player's own.

The tech bet — answered

The shirt-lift concealed draw is genuinely novel — no shipping VR game does grabbable worn cloth (all "clothing physics" out there is ambient sway or an invisible holster trigger with zero cloth in the loop). We'd be inventing, not copying. The verdict: a scoped, choreographed hem-grab is feasible TODAY at 90fps — one hero garment, a guided grab (the hand snaps to a pinned hem region, not free-collision anywhere), body mostly still during the draw, ~2–3k-particle cloth (Obi Cloth in Unity has pin-constraints as a first-class primitive; or Chaos panel-cloth + hand-attach in UE5). What stays years away: fully general, free-form "grab any cloth anywhere while sprinting." So don't simulate the whole shirt honestly — stage the reveal. Everything else in ONI is buildable with techniques that already shipped.

Bonus — the drink mechanic is scientifically validated

Slowing time cuts vection and acceleration — the actual drivers of VR nausea — and a dynamic vignette reduces sickness without hurting presence, and players mostly never notice it (Fernandes & Feiner, IEEE 2016, Best Paper). Disguising a comfort layer as in-world fiction is proven, admired design (Metroid Prime's visor, Dead Space's suit UI). The legendary drink is a real, evidence-backed comfort + accessibility system wearing a costume. It's not a gimmick — it's the smartest part of the design.
